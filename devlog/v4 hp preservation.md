This is devlog entry 4, looking at reward function v4. This version took the most testing and deliberation because I needed to make the agent balance speed against health preservation.

The reason this version exists goes back to the problem from v2. The agent would recklessly almost die just to win the battle one move faster, and v3 never fixed it because it only changed move choice and never measured my HP at the end of the battle. That isn't how good Pokemon players play at all. So I added two things to the reward once the enemy reaches 0 HP. First, an HP-retention bonus that rewards finishing the battle with health to spare. Second, I shrank the fast-win bonus from what it was before, because the old speed reward was so large it drowned out the HP bonus completely. Only after reining in the speed reward could the reward for a safe win actually compete with the reward for a fast win.

I also thought it would be useful to let the user decide how risky the agent should be, through an hp_weight value. If hp_weight is set high, around 10, the agent prioritizes surviving over almost everything. An example of that behavior would be a water Pokemon choosing to Leech Seed and Giga Drain a fire Pokemon to stay safe, rather than just Hydro Pumping it and finishing it off. I tested hp_weight at 2 and 10 and tested several values near 6, and the ordering flipped around 5.86, where a fast win and a safe win score nearly identically. That weight pushes the agent to finish quickly without ending the battle on the verge of death.

By design, the agent should stop just spamming the highest-power effective move to win one turn sooner. Instead it should prefer a move that heals itself, or a weaker but still type-effective move that takes no damage in return. By adjusting hp_weight, I can also push it toward riskier or safer play on purpose. I'll confirm these behaviors once I run a full training pass with v4.

This is where it connects to robotics. In the real world, training always involves trade-offs. Here, the trade is safety for speed and the other way around. A real robot might have to trade balance for strength, or power draw for stability. You can't eliminate those trade-offs, you can only design a reward function good enough to balance them deliberately.
