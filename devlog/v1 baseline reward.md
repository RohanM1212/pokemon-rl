This is devlog entry 1, looking at reward function v1. When I started programming this agent, I wanted to begin with the simplest thing that could possibly work. I needed a baseline to see if the agent could even interface with the emulator memory correctly. The math is straightforward. The agent gets rewarded based on how much damage it deals as a percentage of the enemy's max HP, scaled by 2. It gets penalized based on how much damage it takes as a percentage of its own max HP, scaled by 1.5. Winning the match gives a flat +10, losing gives -10, and running out of the max 50 turns gives -2.
The question I was trying to answer was whether a completely naive reward signal, just damage in versus damage out, would produce any intelligent behavior at all. Would the network actually figure out the game mechanics, or would it just spam random moves because the state space was too overwhelming?
What I expected was pretty simple. I figured the agent would quickly learn to use the highest power move every single turn since that maximizes immediate damage dealt and maximizes the positive scaling factor.
What I found during the training run confirmed exactly that. The model did not do anything fancy. It completely ignores defensive moves, stat changes, or status conditions because those moves deal zero percent damage and yield zero immediate reward points on that turn. If an attack did heavy raw damage, the agent clicked it over and over. It just spammed the strongest attack and completely forgot how to do anything else. It worked for winning basic, straightforward matchups, but it was not smart.
The thing is, v1 has absolutely no concept of urgency. To the algorithm, a win is a win. The agent gets the exact same reward whether it finishes off the enemy in 3 turns or drags the struggle out for 49 turns. Because there is no time pressure, the agent settles for highly inefficient loops as long as the net damage stays positive. That lack of urgency is what is holding it back, and that becomes the main motivation for v2.
